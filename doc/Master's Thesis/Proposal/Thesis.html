Terrainosaurus
Realistic Terrain Synthesis Via Genetic Algorithms on GIS Elevation Data
Thesis Proposal
Ryan L. Saunders
Dr. John Keyser (advisor)

Introduction

Computer-rendered terrain is an important facet of most applications that attempt to represent the natural world in some way, including geographic information systems (GIS) applications, CG-animated feature films, virtual reality systems, and computer games. The ability to synthesize large-scale, realistic terrain models would be of considerable interest for these applications (with the exception of the GIS-related applications, which are generally only interested in representing faithfully the earth's landscape as it actually is).

Much of the recent research in virtual terrain has centered around accelerating the visualization of large terrains to achieve interactive frame rates, resulting in a wide array of level-of-detail (LOD) algorithms, which are methods of increasing rendering speed through omitting fine detail that would be indiscernible to the viewer [Duchaineau et al.  1997] [Ulrich 2002] [Losasso and Hoppe 2004] [Li et al.  2003] . Recent, dramatic advances in the performance and capabilities of graphics accelerators have enabled the presentation of richly-detailed terrain models at interactive frame-rates, and have sparked increased interest in representing outdoor phenomena, as evidenced by the prominence of nature-related techniques at a recent Game Developer's Conference [Sanchez-Crespo 2002] . The visually stunning outdoor scenes in the recent video game title Halo are solid evidence that believable terrain can be achieved with current technology [Halo 2001] .

Terrain generation, in contrast, has received relatively little treatment in the literature. The most prevalent techniques are fractal-based, because they are easily implemented, require relatively little processing time and human input, and yield at least mediocre results, allowing random, unique terrains to be generated on-the-fly. However, as Losasso & Hoppe [Losasso and Hoppe 2004] and Pelton & Atkinson [Pelton and Atkinson 2003] have noted, fractal methods for terrain generation are limited in the types of terrain they can simulate, and one generally has to resort to GIS elevation maps to get more interesting and believable terrain models.

In this proposal I describe a new methodology for terrain generation, which I expect will be able to synthesize realistic terrain models composed of a variety of terrain types. In this approach, GIS elevation data is fed as raw input to a genetic algorithm which generates unique, synthetic terrain models that are similar in their relevant characteristics to the input elevation data. My approach should be able to handle any type (or combination of types) of terrain, and at multiple levels of detail, up to the resolution of the input elevation data. Finer detail could be synthesized atop the GIS-based terrain through the use of existing techniques such as detail-map textures and fractal noise). The user is able to specify the shape and placement of various regions of terrain via a CAD-style map-drawing interface, which could be extended to permit a finer degree of user control (such as choosing the placement of individual features).

Idealized Terrain Generation

Before I turn to the task of describing my approach to terrain generation, it is worth spending some time on the question of what makes an algorithm for terrain generation "good". What features would characterize an "ideal" methodology?

As is the case with many difficult problems, in terrain generation we are confronted with a number of parameters that we would like to optimize, but which are in tension with one another. An ideal terrain generation algorithm would:

  • require a low degree of human input
  • allow a high degree of human control
  • be able to reproduce a wide variety of recognizable terrain types and features in believable relationship to one another
  • produce models at arbitrary levels of detail
  • run quickly enough to be used in real-time, dynamic applications
  • be extensible to support new terrain types and features

Obviously, accomplishing all of these goals simultaneously is probably impossible. Nevertheless, having such an ideal in view gives us a yardstick with which to evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of the various approaches to terrain synthesis.

Low Degree of Human Input

Digital terrain models typically involve large amounts of data. For example, a standard 10-meter USGS DEM (digital elevation model) [USGS 2003] contains more than 10,000 data points per square kilometer. If an artist is required to be involved in the determination of each data point, this adds up to an enormous amount of painstaking work on the part of the artist. Any terrain construction detail that can be offloaded from the artist to the algorithm should result in a corresponding increase in human productivity. An ideal generation methodology would accept a request such as "randomly generate a 1000x1000-point desert terrain", and produce a believable result.

If the inputs required by an algorithm are sufficiently minimal, it may afford, in addition to the increase in artist productivity, a second benefit: the algorithm may be useful for generating unique terrains at run-time (e.g., as a random level generator for a game).

High Degree of Human Control

In obvious tension with the previous ideal is the goal of permitting the artist to exert an arbitrarily fine degree of control over the features and characteristics of the generated terrain. To give an artist full creative freedom requires that he be able to specify both low-frequency features (hills, valleys, mountains, etc.) and high-frequency features (cracks, cliffs, and gorges), at both global and local levels. An ideal algorithm would permit the artist to exert whatever control he wished, where he wished, while intelligently filling in the details the artist doesn't care about.

Diverse, Believable Features and Terrain Types

A terrain generation algorithm that is able to believably produce only a narrow range of terrain types and features will inherently be limited in its usefulness. An ideal algorithm would be able to create a wide diversity of terrain types (e.g., desert, mountains, plains) and features (e.g. ravines, riverbeds, volcanoes), both from the real world and from the imagination of the artist. Additionally, the seams where terrain types meet (e.g., the transition from mountains to foothills) would be believable, and the placement of semantic features would make sense (e.g., waterfalls would pour into pools, rivers would not flow uphill).

Arbitrary Level-of-Detail

It is precisely because terrain models involve such large quantities of data that continuous level of detail (CLOD) algorithms are necessary for their visualization. But full-detail terrain models are not always needed, the requisite level-of-detail being determined by their intended use. For example, a model meant to be a backdrop in a computer animated film would need highly realistic detail only in the areas where the action was to take place, and could use relatively simple geometry in the other areas. Similarly, the level of detail required for the terrain of a flight simulation is dramatically different than what is needed for ground-level action. An ideal terrain generation algorithm would permit the creation of terrains at multiple levels of detail, and the segmentation of large terrains into regions of differing level-of-detail.

Fast Enough for Real-Time Applications

Some terrain-processing algorithms require a lengthy preprocessing phase [Ulrich 2002] [L3DT ] before the terrain can be used in a real-time application. Intuitively, we would expect all but the most simplistic terrain generation algorithms to fall into this category. Nonetheless, an algorithm that could do all or most of its processing "on the fly", would provide several important advantages, including:

  • instantaneous feedback to the artist of the effects of a particular modification
  • the ability to modify dynamically the terrain in fundamental and interesting ways (such as transforming a mountain into a crater, or rapidly eroding a riverbed into a deep gorge)
  • enormous savings in the amount of memory and disk storage required (if the terrain could be generated from a small set of parameters)
  • the ability to create seamless, infinite worlds (if these parameters are defined at every point in the virtual world)

Related Work

Terrain Representation, Visualization & Use

The majority of the terrain generation and visualization literature is concerned with heightfields, 2D rectangular grids of elevation data. Other representations of terrain are certainly possible, but heightfields offer several nice advantages that make them popular.

Conceptually, heightfields are very similar to grayscale images. As a result, a heightfield terrain representation is convenient because it enables existing image-based methods for creation, manipulation, compression, storage and analysis to be reused for terrain.

The regular grid structure of the heightfield is central to many of the existing continuous level of detail (CLOD) algorithms, which render portions of the terrain near the viewer with many triangles and far-off portions with fewer triangles [Duchaineau et al.  1997] , [Losasso and Hoppe 2004] .

Heightfields are restricted to depicting terrains in which elevation is a function of the grid coordinates. In other words, the terrain must pass the vertical line test: features like overhangs and caves, where a vertical line would intersect the surface multiple times, cannot be represented directly with heightfields. In practice, this limitation is not especially cumbersome, as most terrain is fairly well-behaved and exceptions (such as the aforementioned caves and overhangs) can be handled as special cases. Furthermore, the simplicity of a 2D representation for terrain allows operations like path-finding and collision detection to be simplified.

Methods of Terrain Generation

Terrain generation has not received nearly as much attention in the literature as terrain LOD rendering has, with relatively few innovations to date. The terrain generation methods that currently exist in the literature generally fall into one or more of the following four categories. After considering the various strengths and weaknesses of each of these approaches, I will briefly survey current software packages for terrain generation, paying attention to which of these techniques they use.

GIS-based Methods

Probably the most obvious place to look for natural-looking terrain data is in nature itself. High-quality elevation maps of the entire United States (and some other parts of the world) can be downloaded or ordered from the U.S. Geological Survey [USGS 2004] and other mapping agencies, at resolutions as high as 3 inches per sample in some cases, and in several formats (older elevation maps are available in the older DEM format [USGS 2003] , with more recent data available only in the more complex SDTS format [Rinehart 2003] ). GIS data is ideal for applications whose primary concern is realism, such as cartographic software [ArcGIS ] , and simulations [SimCity 4 2003] , [Flight Simulator 2004 ] . This approach has the advantage of producing a high degree of realism with relatively little human effort. The major drawback to GIS technology is the constraint that it imposes: only real-world locations that are present in a mapping agency's data can be used. For many applications, this constraint may unacceptably compromise artistic freedom (e.g., a movie director may require a particular arrangement of mountains and rivers) or other, more utilitarian goals (e.g., the setting for a military simulation might require particular terrain features). A second disadvantage of GIS-based methods is the significant amount of space required to store a large dataset.

My approach improves upon the naïve GIS-based approach, retaining its strength (high degree of realism for a low expenditure of human effort) while circumventing its primary drawback (static terrain configuration).

Procedural Methods

Procedural methods for terrain generation date back as far as 1977 [Parry 1977] [Marshall et al.  1980] , and are largely the domain of artists and game developers [Fernandez ] [Woodhouse 2003] , and are generally optimized for speed and/or ease of implementation rather than for realism. Procedural techniques almost always incorporate a random noise generator at some point and (ideally) produce unique results each time. And since the random noise generator usually provides most or all of the information needed to build the terrain, landscapes constructed via procedural methods usually require little or no human input, and can be of arbitrary levels of detail. Additionally, if the procedure is fast enough, random terrain models can be created at application startup (or even on-the-fly), obviating the need to store any heightfield data at all. As a result, procedural techniques are ideal for generating random terrains and infinite worlds. The principal drawback of these methods is that they generally have no causal connection to the real-world terrain they try to simulate, instead bearing only an incidental resemblance (in other words, they only happen to look terrain-like rather than being somehow derived from actual terrain). Because of this, most procedural methods are only useful for simulating a limited range of terrain types. In addition, since the procedural parameters do not typically correspond to real-world terrain characteristics, achieving a desired effect is likely to be an exercise in trial and error.

Some of the most popular procedural techniques are fractal in nature, such as the midpoint displacement method [Fournier et al.  1982] and Perlin's noise function [Ebert et al.  1998] . These techniques exhibit the fractal property of self-similarity at different scales, and generally involve perturbing the height values of the terrain by increasingly smaller amounts at increasingly finer scales. Prusinkiewicz & Hammel describe an interesting method for generating mountains with an integrated river network that is entirely fractal in nature [Prusinkiewicz and Hammel 1993] .

A similar class of procedural techniques in common practice could be described as "collaging" methods [Burke 1996] . In these techniques, an irregular heightfield is created through the random superposition of simple shapes, such as spheres, cones, half-planes, or 2D trigonometric functions. After several hundred iterations, the result is a random heightfield possessing similar curvature and smoothness characteristics to the primitive shape, but without any of the primitive shapes apparent in the terrain.

The spectral synthesis family of procedural techniques involves constructing a frequency-spectrum representation of a terrain and performing the inverse FFT or inverse wavelet transform to produce a spatial-domain terrain [Pelton and Atkinson 2003] [Franke 2000] . In the simplest case, the frequency components would be calculated according to some random noise function. Pelton & Atkinson describe a wavelet synthesis approach based on the frequency histogram of a GIS elevation map, noting that their approach is successful at creating a unique heightfield with similar roughness characteristics to the original, but that it fails to capture semantic features, specifically riverbeds [Pelton and Atkinson 2003] .

My approach is similar to existing procedural methods in that it iteratively constructs unique landscapes using controlled random processes, but unlike the methods discussed above, it also tries to analyze its performance and to weed out unsuccessful attempts.

Sculpture-based Methods

At the opposite end of the spectrum, in terms of human effort, is the use of artistic tools to paint or sculpt the features of the terrain. In most cases, this means using computer-based modeling and painting tools (such as Adobe Photoshop, Bryce 3D, or Maya) or specialized "level editors" (as in the recent games SimCity 4 [SimCity 4 2003] and Unreal Tournament 2004 [Unreal Tournament 2004 ] ). Less commonly, an artist might create a model using traditional physical media and digitize it using laser scanning techniques, as was done to create the terrain for the video game title Trespasser [Wyckoff 1999] . The primary advantage of sculpture-based methods is the enormous freedom given to the artist. Anything the artist can imagine can be created. This is also its main drawback — this method of terrain generation requires a large investment of human time and effort to produce good results.

My approach allows a human designer to influence the shape and features of the terrain by specifying the relationships between different types of terrain, but does not rely on the designer for determining the high-frequency details of the model.

Simulation-based Methods

Another family of terrain generation methods involves the simulation of physical processes (such as plate tectonics or erosion) to evolve the terrain [Burke 1996] [Kelley et al.  1988] [Musgrave et al.  1989] . These methods can produce highly believable results, since they emulate the same processes that create real-world terrain, but typically require very high amounts of processing time.

Existing Terrain Generation Tools

While the list of terrain generation engines is too large to be reviewed exhaustively, it is worthwhile to make note of a representative sample of the more prominent of these. Most of these applications permit the use of more than one of the above techniques as a way of mitigating some of the weaknesses of each.

Terragen

Terragen is a well-known terrain generation and rendering tool, developed by Planetside Software, free for non-commercial use. It provides several fractal functions for generating terrains and also provides a simple set of sculpting tools for modifying the resultant heightfields. It offers an integrated ray tracing engine including some very nice cloud, water, and atmospheric lighting effects, and has a significant artistic community [Terragen ] .

MojoWorld

MojoWorld is a set of applications for creating and exploring fractal worlds. MojoWorld Transporter is a free program for exploring the worlds created by the commercial product, MojoWorld Generator. Unlike fractal/sculpting heightfield generators, MojoWorld handles fractal objects in symbolic form, allowing entire worlds to be viewed at infinite levels of detail and stored in relatively small files [MojoWorld ] .

Bryce 3D

Bryce 3D is a general-purpose 3D modeling and animation tool, but is best known for its terrain modeling capabilities. It offers a full set of painting tools for generating and editing heightfield-based terrain objects, including the ability to use Adobe Photoshop image filters [Bryce 3D ] .

World Machine

World Machine is a procedural heightfield generation tool, free for non-commercial use. Its most interesting aspect is that it treats the terrain creation process as composed of simple "device" primitives (e.g., Perlin noise generator, Gaussian blur filter, etc.) which the user can connect to form a "machine" (a directed acyclic graph) that will produce the heightfield. The main benefit of this approach over a more traditional fractal/sculpting approach is that since the series of operations is preserved as a DAG, the user is free to tweak any parameter of any operation at any time, as opposed to having to commit to a particular filter size or random seed at the outset [World Machine ] .

Genetic Algorithms

Unfortunately, some of the more interesting problems in computer science fall into the class of "NP hard" problems, and become intractably difficult as the size of the problem increases [Cormen et al.  2001] . In these problems, it is often not feasible to find a perfect solution, and approximation techniques must be used. Genetic algorithms (GA) is one such class of techniques.

Conceptually, a genetic algorithm tries to evolve better and better solutions to its problem through mutation and "breeding". In GA terms, a single possible solution within the solution space of the problem is called an individual or a chromosome. A fitness function provides an evaluation of how good a solution any particular individual is, expressed as a scalar value. As the GA iterates, new potential solutions to the problem are explored by taking the more "fit" of the existing individuals and combining parts of them (their genes) to create new individuals having characteristics of both "parents". By iteratively evolving a population (set of individuals) of some fixed size, the GA explores the solution space in a parallel fashion. Hopefully, after some reasonable number of iterations, the most "fit" of the individuals in the final population will be fairly close to an optimal solution [Obitko and Skavík 1999] .

My approach to terrain generation uses genetic algorithms at two stages. An appropriately designed fitness function will be able to discriminate between plausible, natural-looking terrains and those which do not appear natural. By incorporating this fitness evaluation as a sort of intelligent feedback loop in the generation process, I expect to be able to synthesize new, unique terrain models that still maintain the qualities of the original GIS data.

Proposed Thesis Research

Overview

I propose a new methodology for terrain construction based on the use of genetic algorithms to synthesize terrain using GIS elevation data as input. Fundamental to the approach is the concept of a terrain type, which I define as a class of terrain features with semantic meaning to the user (e.g., "desert", "steep mountains", "rolling hills", etc.) and one or more associated sample GIS heightfield samples typical of that terrain type. The samples within a single terrain type should bear resemblance to one another in some set of measurable characteristics (i.e., one instance of "glacier" should have similar characteristics to another instance of the same type, and both should have dissimilar parameters to an instance of "desert"). These terrain types will be used as building blocks by the user to create arbitrarily complex terrains.

At a high level, the approach consists of an optional user input step, followed by two genetic algorithm steps. Starting with one or more predefined terrain types, the user is allowed to specify the general desired configuration by drawing a rough, 2D map of connected polygonal regions and specifying the terrain type of each region. The algorithm then generates more complex, natural-looking boundaries approximating the straight lines of the polygonal map, in order to avoid the presence of unrealistic straight lines in the output heightfield. Finally, it constructs a heightfield of the requested size and shape by splatting together compatible chunks of terrain data from the GIS samples. A population of such landscapes will be generated, and the most fit of the population will be returned as the generated terrain heightfield.

User Input

In the optional user input phase, the user is given the opportunity to specify the terrain layout as a map (if this phase is omitted, the generated terrain will have only one region of a single terrain type). The user draws the 2D map as connected polygonal regions using a CAD-style editor GUI and specifies the terrain type of each polygonal region. This map can have an arbitrary number of regions, each with an arbitrarily complicated boundary, allowing the user to exert an arbitrary degree of control over the shape of a terrain by creating custom-shaped regions of different terrain types. The user may save a map that he has created to a file and may load it again at a later time.

The inputs expected from the user are intentionally primitive, as a convenience for the user — in the next step, the straight edges created by the user will be approximated by more complex, more believable boundaries. In the vast majority of cases, the user is not going to care about the exact shape of the boundary, only that it is plausible and that it goes approximately where he asked it to go. In the case where the user actually wants fine-grained control over the exact shape of the boundary, he can simply draw the boundary with more line segments — the shorter each segment is, the more closely the approximation will adhere to it.

Genetic Boundary Refinement

The first genetic algorithm step consists of a 2D refinement of the edges in the user created polygonal map (if any exist). I call the simple edges created by the user guide boundaries. The purpose of this step is to convert these simple, low-detail edges given by the user into refined boundaries, irregular, high-detail boundaries. Whereas the guide boundary was a single, longer line segment, the refined version is a piecewise-linear curve composed of numerous short line segments connecting the original two endpoints of the guide boundary, but in a meandering, undulating fashion. The length and number of the line segments used to construct the refined boundary is effectively limited by the spatial resolution of the input heightfields, as detail finer than the resolution of the output heightfields will not appear in the output terrain.

Genetic Heightfield Construction

The second genetic algorithm step takes the refined map and generates a heightfield conforming to it. There are several challenges to be overcome in order to do this. First, the heightfield construction problem must be phrased as a genetic algorithm. This requires some way of transforming a heightfield into a chromosome and vice versa. It also requires a fitness function for evaluating how viable a particular chromosome/heightfield is. This fitness function will need to consider a variety of factors when assessing a heightfield, including how well each region conforms to its terrain type, how well terrain types meet at their boundaries, and how well features such as ridge-lines and valleys cohere throughout the whole heightfield. Evaluating terrain type conformity will likely involve image processing techniques such as ridge detection, gradient analysis and frequency-spectrum analysis. After observing the failure of their wavelet synthesis approach to reproduce features such as riverbeds, Pelton & Atkinson state, "We believe that another transformation could more accurately describe the terrain features caused by erosion. A system that generates/analyzes terrain in the Fourier space could possibly capture these missing features" [Pelton and Atkinson 2003] .

Analysis

The approach outlined above does not fit cleanly into any of the categories defined earlier, but incorporates aspects of several of them. While it is clearly procedural in nature, it is unlike the procedural methods discussed so far in that it possesses a degree of decision-making intelligence as a result of the genetic algorithm. It is a GIS-based method, but uses the GIS data as raw input rather than as the output terrain itself. It is similar to a sculpting method in that it permits an arbitrary degree of user interaction, but allows the user to decide how much oversight he wants to exercise. As a result, my approach combines many of the strengths of the existing methods, and is likely to produce realistic results for less human effort. The major drawback of this approach is its efficiency: due to the nature of genetic algorithms, it is almost certain to require lots of processing time, and may have high memory requirements as well.
